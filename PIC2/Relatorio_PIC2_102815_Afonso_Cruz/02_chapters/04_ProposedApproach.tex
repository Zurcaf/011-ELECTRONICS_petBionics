\chapter{Proposed Approach}

\section{System Overview}

The proposed system consists of an instrumented canine prosthesis designed to
acquire biomechanical data during locomotion in real-world conditions.
The system integrates inertial and force sensing, embedded processing, and
local data storage into a compact and low-power architecture.

Unlike traditional gait analysis systems based on force plates or motion
capture, which require controlled environments and specialised equipment, this
approach enables continuous and unobtrusive monitoring during normal animal
activity~\cite{McLaughlin2001}.
The system is therefore designed with a strong emphasis on portability, energy
efficiency, and long-term data acquisition.

\section{Instrumented Prosthesis Concept}

The prosthesis acts simultaneously as a mobility aid and as a sensing platform.
Sensors are embedded within the prosthetic structure, enabling continuous data
acquisition without altering the natural gait of the animal.

By embedding sensing capabilities directly into the prosthesis, the system
enables the objective and repeatable assessment of prosthetic adaptation
identified as missing in Chapter~\ref{chapter:background}.

A notable advantage of this design is that all sensing components are entirely
non-invasive and external to the animal: no electrodes, pressure cuffs, or
skin-contact sensors are required. The instrumentation is confined to the
prosthetic device itself, significantly simplifying clinical deployment and
reducing implementation cost compared to approaches that require attaching
sensors directly to the animal's body.

\section{Sensor Integration}

The sensing architecture combines an inertial measurement unit (IMU),
specifically the MPU-9250, with a load sensing chain composed of an inline
load cell (LCR02) and an HX711 24-bit analog-to-digital converter.

The IMU provides tri-axial acceleration, angular velocity, and magnetometer
measurements, enabling the extraction of temporal gait parameters such as
stride segmentation and stance and swing phases.
Wearable IMU-based systems have been shown to provide accurate gait
characterisation while maintaining portability~\cite{Jenkins2018,Zhang2022}.

The load cell enables direct measurement of axial forces transmitted through
the prosthesis, which is critical for assessing weight-bearing behaviour and
prosthesis acceptance~\cite{McLaughlin2001,Budsberg1996}.
The combination of kinematic and kinetic sensing provides a more complete
biomechanical characterisation than either modality alone.

The system sampling rate is 80\,Hz, constrained by the HX711 output data rate.
This is sufficient for the target application, as canine stride frequencies are
typically 2--4\,Hz~\cite{Hayati2019,Jenkins2018}, providing more than 20
samples per gait cycle.

\section{Embedded System Architecture}

The system is built around the Seeed Studio XIAO ESP32-C3 microcontroller,
selected for its integration of wireless communication (Wi-Fi and BLE), low
power operation, and compact form factor~\cite{ESP32C3}.

The architecture follows a local-first data acquisition strategy: data is
continuously acquired and stored locally on a microSD card, and only
transferred via wireless communication when explicitly requested.
This avoids the high energy cost of continuous data transmission and ensures
robustness when connectivity is intermittent~\cite{Wang2019,Bulling2014}.

The system operates in two primary modes:
(i)~\textbf{acquisition mode}, in which sensors are active and data is
stored locally with wireless communication disabled, for minimal power
consumption; and
(ii)~\textbf{transfer mode}, in which Wi-Fi is enabled for data offloading
via a browser-accessible web interface.

The power system uses two 250\,mAh LiPo cells in parallel (500\,mAh total
at 3.7\,V)~\cite{LiPo250mAh}, providing an estimated autonomy of approximately
12--16\,h in acquisition mode (35--55\,mA) and 6--8\,h with Wi-Fi active
(100--175\,mA).

\section{Single-Limb Instrumentation Strategy}

The current implementation adopts a single-limb instrumentation strategy,
embedding sensing capabilities exclusively within the prosthetic limb.
This design significantly reduces system complexity, cost, and intrusiveness,
while still enabling extraction of meaningful gait parameters.

Previous studies have demonstrated that limb-mounted IMUs can accurately
capture temporal gait features such as stride timing and phase
transitions~\cite{Jenkins2018,Trojaniello2014}.
The proposed approach prioritises scalability and usability, particularly for
long-term monitoring~\cite{Zhang2022}.

While single-limb instrumentation is sufficient to characterise the behaviour
of the prosthetic limb in isolation, extending the system with additional IMUs
on the remaining limbs would enable inter-limb coordination analysis and a more
complete characterisation of whole-body gait.
This multi-limb extension is considered a natural evolution of the proposed
architecture and is planned for a future development phase.

\section{Expected Contribution}

The proposed system provides a portable and objective method for evaluating
prosthetic adaptation in canine patients.
By combining embedded sensing, local data acquisition, and post-processing,
the system enables a transition from subjective assessment methods to
quantitative and repeatable metrics.

The main contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Continuous kinematic and kinetic monitoring in real-world conditions;
  \item Integration of IMU and force sensing in a single compact device
        embedded within the prosthesis;
  \item A fully non-invasive design requiring no sensors on the animal's body,
        simplifying clinical deployment and reducing implementation cost;
  \item A single-limb instrumentation strategy extensible to multi-limb
        configurations for whole-body gait analysis;
  \item An energy-efficient architecture suitable for extended monitoring sessions.
\end{itemize}
