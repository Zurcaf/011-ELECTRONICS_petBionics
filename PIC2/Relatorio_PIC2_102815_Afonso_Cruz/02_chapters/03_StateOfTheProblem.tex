\chapter{State of the Problem}

\section{Current Approaches in Canine Gait Assessment}

Existing methods for gait analysis in animals can be broadly divided into laboratory-based systems, wearable systems, and vision-based approaches.

Laboratory-based systems, such as force plates and pressure walkways, provide accurate measurements of ground reaction forces and spatial parameters \cite{McLaughlin2001,Oosterlinck2011}. However, these systems require controlled environments and are not suitable for continuous monitoring in real-world conditions.

Wearable systems, typically based on inertial sensors, enable data collection during natural movement \cite{Jenkins2018,Ladha2017,Zhang2022}. These systems are more flexible and suitable for real-world deployment, but may be limited in the type and accuracy of measurements they provide, particularly with respect to load quantification.

Vision-based approaches rely on video analysis to extract motion parameters. While useful, these methods often require complex setups and are sensitive to environmental conditions, limiting their applicability outside laboratory environments.

\section{Instrumented Systems for Locomotion Analysis}

Recent work has explored the use of instrumented systems combining inertial and force sensing to analyze locomotion, aiming to provide a more complete description of movement by capturing both kinematic and kinetic data \cite{Zhang2022}.

In the context of veterinary applications, such systems have been used to detect lameness, characterize gait patterns, and monitor rehabilitation processes \cite{Fahie2018}. However, their integration into prosthetic devices remains limited, and their use is often constrained to controlled experimental setups.

\section{Limitations of Existing Solutions}

Despite significant advances, current solutions present several limitations. Laboratory-based systems lack portability and cannot be used in everyday environments. Wearable systems often rely on multiple sensors distributed across limbs, which increases system complexity and reduces practicality for long-term use.

Additionally, most systems are designed for short-term analysis and do not support continuous data acquisition over extended periods. The lack of integration with prosthetic devices further limits their applicability in assessing prosthesis adaptation.

Furthermore, many existing approaches rely on symmetry-based metrics, which require simultaneous measurement of multiple limbs \cite{Oosterlinck2011,Fahie2018}. This assumption is not valid in prosthetic applications where only the prosthetic limb can be instrumented.

These limitations highlight that current solutions are not designed for real-world deployment in prosthetic applications, where simplicity, robustness, and minimal instrumentation are critical.

\section{Research Gap}

The analysis of existing approaches reveals a clear gap in the development of portable, integrated, and continuous monitoring systems for prosthetic evaluation in animals.

There is a need for a solution capable of acquiring relevant biomechanical data directly from the prosthetic limb, operating in real-world conditions, and providing objective metrics for assessing adaptation.

This work aims to address this gap by proposing an instrumented prosthesis combined with data-driven analysis techniques, enabling continuous and objective assessment of prosthesis adaptation directly from the prosthetic limb in real-world conditions.

Unlike existing approaches, the proposed system focuses on single-limb instrumentation while preserving both kinetic and kinematic information, providing a practical and scalable solution for real-world prosthetic evaluation.
